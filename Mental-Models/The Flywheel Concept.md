# The Flywheel Concept

## Idea
Starting is the hardest part — once momentum builds, progress becomes self-sustaining and hard to stop.

## Definition
In mechanical engineering, a flywheel is a rotating wheel that stores rotational energy. It requires enormous effort to get spinning but is equally hard to stop once moving. Applied to life and work: the early stages of any endeavor (a blog, a business, a habit) feel pointless — no audience, no results, no feedback. Most people quit here. Those who persist past this phase reach a tipping point where the flywheel's own momentum carries them forward.

The practical implication: *getting started* is the bottleneck. Once you sit down, lace up, or open the laptop, the rest tends to follow.

## Source
Mechanical engineering analogy; personal observation on habit formation.

> "It is just a matter of getting the flywheel started."

## Compass

**Roots** — *where this comes from*
This belongs to the family of [[Mental Models]] around compounding and momentum — the non-obvious idea that most worthwhile things have a long, unrewarding ramp before they start paying off. The open question it raises: what is the minimum viable push to get the flywheel moving at all?

**Paths** — *where this leads*
Once you accept the flywheel, the natural next question is *how hard to push* — and [[85% Rule]] suggests the answer is not as hard as you think. Sustainable effort beats maximum effort because the flywheel doesn't need peak force, it needs [[Consistency]]. This also connects to [[Compound Effect]] — every session adds energy that doesn't dissipate, which means the returns accelerate the longer you stay in the game.

**Neighbors** — *what lives nearby*
The flywheel is really a physics metaphor for [[Consistency]] — small, repeated inputs that individually feel meaningless but accumulate into something unstoppable. [[Consistency > Intensity]] captures the same truth from a different angle: it's not about heroic effort, it's about showing up. And [[The Parable of the Pottery Class]] is the canonical story of this — the students who made more pots made better pots, because repetition compounds in ways that planning never can.

**Clash** — *what pushes against this*
[[Laziness]] is the static friction that keeps the flywheel dead still — the gap between knowing you should start and actually starting. The deeper enemy is the expectation of immediate results, which makes the early thankless phase feel like proof that it's not working rather than the necessary cost of getting the wheel to turn.
